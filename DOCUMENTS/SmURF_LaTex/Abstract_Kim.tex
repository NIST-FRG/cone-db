\section{Abstract}
The cone calorimeter is a bench-scale instrument developed by NIST's Fire Research Division in 1982 to address a critical gap in fire science: the lack of reliable tools to measure material flammability based on heat release rate (HRR). Heat release rate is considered the most important indicator of a material's flammability, as it directly governs fire growth rate and overall fire severity. The cone calorimeter works by exposing a sample placed beneath a cone-shaped radiant heater to an external heat flux. Typically 35 kW/m², or up to 50 kW/m² for more fire-resistant materials. Combustion products are then collected through an instrumented exhaust system, and HRR is calculated from the oxygen consumed during burning, based on the principle that heat release is directly proportional to oxygen consumption. Key outputs include time to ignition, mass-loss rate, peak HRR, and total heat released. Because the test simulates forced, radiant ignition, results are most often interpreted as representing the flammability of a material acting as a secondary fuel item in a developing fire. Since becoming commercially available in the mid-1980s, the cone calorimeter has been adopted in more than 300 laboratories worldwide, making it the most widely used bench-scale tool for assessing material flammability.

Over the past 40+ years, NIST researchers have conducted thousands of cone calorimeter tests spanning a broad range of materials, such as Red Oak, PMMA plastics, and other combustible materials, with results published across numerous NIST technical and interagency reports. However, this data has never been consolidated into a single accessible resource. The goal of the ENFP 429 research effort this spring is to compile, review, and organize this historical dataset into a unified database, enabling researchers, engineers, and fire protection professionals to query HRR data across materials, compare flammability characteristics, and apply these benchmarks to fire modeling, material selection, and the design of fire protection systems.
