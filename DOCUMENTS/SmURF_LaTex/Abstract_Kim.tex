\section{Abstract}
The accurate measurement of the volume, heat release, and toxic gas yield of a fire is a keystone in the fire protection and NIST research industry. This semester in ENFP 429 NIST research class, I looked through cone calorimetry data. a bench-scale instrument developed by NIST's Fire Research Division in 1982 to address this issue. The cone calorimeter works by exposing a sample material placed beneath a cone-shaped radiant heater to an external heat flux. Combustion products are then collected through an instrumented exhaust system, and HRR is calculated from the oxygen consumed during burning, based on the principle that heat release is directly proportional to oxygen consumption. 
The significance of this measurement, mainly the material’s heat release rate, allows researchers to find the critical heat flux for specific materials. By calculating the Thermal Response Parameter (TRP), researchers can correlate the time to ignition to the incident radiant heat flux and surface heat losses using the relationship.
t_ign = [ TRP / (q_r - q_loss) ]²
From this, researchers can determine exactly what heat flux can be applied to a material without it igniting. Ultimately, these results are instrumental in resolving debates surrounding building fires and help drive innovations in the fire protection industry.

\section{Disscussion: Systematic Review of User Files (SmURF)}

The reports I Smurfed this semester looked at Red Oak, PMMA, and Upholstery as tested materials. Specifically, in NBSIR 82-2611, Development of the Cone Calorimeter, A Bench-Scale Heat Release Rate Apparatus Based on Oxygen Consumption by Vytenis Babrauskas, looked at solid materials, PMMA and Red Oak. These solid materials were systematically tested under varying external irradiances: 25, 50, and 75 kW/m² and in both horizontal and vertical orientations to observe varying heat release profiles and charring behaviors. In this report, I forked, cloned, and successfully SmURFed 15 PMMA tests and 18 Red Oak tests. The report by Babrauskas was one of the earliest tests created and therefore did not reference a specific number of tests, but through reverse engineering, based on when the data was created, Ryan and I were able to SmURFed all the tests for this report. 
The next report I looked at was "Prediction of Upholstered Chair Heat Release Rates from Bench-Scale Measurements" by Vytenis Babrauskas and John F. Krasny. Of the approximately 63 expected bench-scale cone calorimeter experiments outlined in the original study, 44 test files have been successfully located and SmURFed. These recovered files capture horizontal orientations across 25, 30, 40, and 50 kW/m² heat fluxes for various upholstery composites, specifically testing combinations of fire-retardant (FR) and non-FR polyurethane foam or cotton batting paired with cotton or polyolefin upholstery fabrics.

Of the approximately 63 expected bench-scale cone calorimeter experiments outlined in the original study, 44 test files have been successfully located and processed. These recovered files capture horizontal orientations across 25, 30, 40, and 50 kW/m² heat fluxes for various upholstery composites, specifically testing combinations of fire-retardant (FR) and non-FR polyurethane foam or cotton batting paired with cotton or polyolefin upholstery fabrics.

The bulk of the successfully processed data belongs to upholstery specimen F25 (Standard PU Foam with a Polyolefin cover), with 12 complete files across multiple replicates and heat fluxes. Other well-documented upholstery assemblies now include F21 (9 files), F24 (6 files), and F27 (5 files), as well as the initial datasets for F22 and F23. Currently, there are no processed files listed for specimens F28 or FB. Additionally, several files represent the portion of data that requires further troubleshooting or remains incomplete in the repository. For instance, a significant cluster of tests under the FC upholstery configuration (including replicates for 25, 30, and 40 kW/m² heat fluxes) is currently marked for re-evaluation, and several replicates for F27 (tests 0169 through 0198) have yet to be fully integrated into the finalized dataset.
